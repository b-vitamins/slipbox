#+TITLE: NvL Fixed Points
#+FILETAGS: :physics:spof:ch6:rg:nvl:fixedpoint:
#+SETUPFILE: ~/.config/emacs/setup/setupfile.org

The NvL recursion relation [[id:4d8f9c3e-7a2b-5f1c-8e4d-7a2b5f1c8e4d][Convergence Properties]] exhibits three fixed points that organize the phase diagram and determine critical behavior. At high temperature, K*=0 represents complete disorder. The linearization K' ≈ 2K(2/4)² = K/2 confirms stability—the fixed point attracts all high-temperature flows with eigenvalue 1/2 under the b=√3 transformation.

At low temperature, K*→∞ represents perfect order. For large K, the recursion K' ≈ 2K demonstrates instability—ordered states grow more ordered under coarse-graining. This fixed point's eigenvalue 2 exceeds unity, driving flows toward increasing order.

The non-trivial fixed point emerges where the recursion function equals unity:

\[\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}} = \frac{e^{3K*} + e^{-K*}}{e^{3K*} + 3e^{-K*}} \Rightarrow \sqrt{2}e^{4K*} + \sqrt{2} = e^{4K*} + 3\]

Solving yields:

\[K* = \frac{1}{4}\ln\left(\frac{3-\sqrt{2}}{\sqrt{2}-1}\right) \approx 0.3356\]

This compares favorably with the exactly known critical value K_c ≈ 0.2747 for the triangular lattice, representing a 22% error—remarkably accurate for a first-order truncation.

Linearizing around the critical fixed point:

\[\left.\frac{\partial K'}{\partial K}\right|_{K*} = 2\left(\frac{e^{4K*}+1}{e^{4K*}+3}\right)^2 + 32K*e^{4K*}\frac{(e^{4K*}+1)}{(e^{4K*}+3)^3} \approx 1.624\]

The eigenvalue exceeds unity, confirming instability as required for a critical point separating ordered and disordered phases. The thermal eigenvalue becomes:

\[b^{y_t} = 1.624 \Rightarrow y_t = \frac{\ln(1.624)}{\ln(\sqrt{3})} \approx 0.883\]

This yields critical exponents ν = 1/y_t ≈ 1.13 (exact: 1) and α = 2-2/y_t ≈ -0.26 (exact: 0), demonstrating reasonable accuracy despite the approximation's uncontrolled nature.

The fixed point structure validates the NvL philosophy [[id:4e7f9d3c-8b5a-6c2f-9d7c-8b5a6c2f9d7c][Approximation Quality]]: even crude approximations capture universal features. The critical fixed point's existence between stable sinks reflects the phase transition's marginal nature—a delicate balance between order and disorder that RG flow analysis naturally reveals. The eigenvalue calculation's sensitivity to the fixed point location explains why improved truncations yield better exponents: small shifts in K* produce large changes in derivatives. This sensitivity also suggests why perturbative improvements converge slowly—the expansion point lies precisely where the system exhibits maximum fluctuations [[id:6d8f9c3e-7b4a-5f2c-8e9d-7b4a5f2c8e9d][Truncation Effects]].
