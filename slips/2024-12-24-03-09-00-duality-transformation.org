#+TITLE: 7.14 Duality Transformation
#+FILETAGS: :physics:spof:ch7:series:duality:transformation:
#+SETUPFILE: ~/.config/emacs/setup/setupfile.org

The Kramers-Wannier duality transformation constitutes a exact mapping between the Ising model at different temperatures, revealing hidden symmetries that constrain the location of critical points and the values of universal amplitude ratios. The transformation relates coupling constants through

\[\mathrm{e}^{-2\tilde{K}} = \tanh K, \quad \text{or equivalently} \quad \tilde{K} = -\frac{1}{2}\ln\tanh K\]

This seemingly abstract relation gains physical clarity through the symmetric form obtained via hyperbolic identities:

\[\sinh 2K \cdot \sinh 2\tilde{K} = 1\]

The symmetry of this expression reveals that duality exchanges strong and weak coupling regimes, mapping ordered phases to disordered ones and vice versa.

The transformation satisfies several remarkable properties that elevate it beyond a mere mathematical curiosity. First, it forms an involution: \(D(D(K)) = K\), ensuring that each temperature has a unique dual partner. This pairing connects high-temperature behavior at \(K\) with low-temperature behavior at \(\tilde{K}\), enabling the calculation of low-temperature properties from high-temperature expansions. Second, the mapping possesses a unique fixed point where \(\tilde{K}_c = K_c\), determining the critical coupling through the self-consistency condition \(\mathrm{e}^{-2K_c} = \tanh K_c\).

The operational mechanism of duality becomes transparent through series expansions [[7.5]]. Low-temperature configurations consist of droplets of overturned spins weighted by \(\exp(-2K \times \text{perimeter})\), while high-temperature graphs form closed loops weighted by \((\tanh K)^{\text{length}}\). The boundaries of droplets correspond precisely to allowed graphs, establishing a one-to-one mapping between expansion terms. The duality transformation ensures that these geometrically identical configurations carry equal statistical weight.

Physical observables transform in specific ways under duality. The free energy acquires only analytic corrections, preserving singular behavior: if \(f(K)\) has a singularity at \(K_c\), then \(f(\tilde{K})\) must be singular at \(\tilde{K}_c = D(K_c)\). Since physical free energies are analytic except at phase transitions, any critical point must map to itself: \(K_c = \tilde{K}_c\). This self-duality condition uniquely determines \(K_c = \frac{1}{2}\ln(\sqrt{2}+1)\) for the square lattice [[7.16]].

The transformation extends beyond the Ising model to other systems with appropriate modifications. The \(q\)-state Potts model exhibits similar dualities, while continuous spin systems like the XY model require more sophisticated treatments. Three-dimensional models generally lack self-duality but possess dual descriptions as gauge theories [[7.6]], demonstrating that duality principles transcend specific dimensions or models. The existence of dualities suggests deep organizational principles in statistical mechanics, connecting seemingly disparate physical regimes through exact mathematical mappings.
