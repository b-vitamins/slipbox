#+TITLE: Operator Mixing Under RG
#+FILETAGS: :physics:criticalphenomena:rg:operators:mixing:eigenvectors:

A subtle but crucial aspect of renormalization group theory is that operators mix under RG transformations. Even if certain operators are absent from the initial Hamiltonian, they are generated by the coarse-graining procedure. This mixing reflects the fact that RG transformations do not preserve the simple product structure of operators.

Consider starting with only $m^2$ and $m^4$ terms. At second order in perturbation theory, coarse graining generates $m^6$ terms from diagrams where six external legs attach to two quartic vertices. The momentum integrals in these diagrams produce not just $m^6$ but also gradient operators like $m^2(\nabla m)^2$ and $m^4(\nabla m)^2$. The expansion of propagators in powers of external momenta, as in

\[\int \frac{\mathrm{d}^{d} \mathbf{k}}{(2 \pi)^{d}} \frac{1}{\left(t+K k^{2}\right)\left(t+K(\mathbf{q}+\mathbf{k})^{2}\right)} = f_0 + f_2 q^2 + \cdots\]

generates an infinite tower of gradient operators.

The mixing means that the RG transformation is not diagonal in the basis of simple operators. The eigendirections of the stability matrix generally involve linear combinations of operators. Near the Gaussian fixed point, the mixing is weak—eigenvectors are approximately aligned with individual operators. But the true scaling fields are these combinations, not the original operators.

For the Wilson-Fisher fixed point, the relevant eigendirection is primarily along $t$ but includes admixtures of all scalar operators: $t + c_1u + c_2u_6 + \cdots$. Similarly, the least irrelevant direction is primarily along $u$ but contains corrections from other operators. These admixtures are of order $\epsilon$ and modify the relationship between bare couplings and scaling fields.

The operator mixing has practical consequences. When measuring critical exponents, one must ensure that the system is tuned along the true relevant direction, not just the bare temperature. The mixing also affects amplitude ratios and correction-to-scaling exponents. However, the universal exponents themselves depend only on eigenvalues, not eigenvectors, making them robust against these complications.

The complete treatment of operator mixing requires working in the full infinite-dimensional space of couplings. The epsilon expansion succeeds because near four dimensions, the mixing between relevant or marginal operators and strongly irrelevant ones is suppressed by large eigenvalue differences. This hierarchy allows truncation to a finite set of operators while maintaining systematic accuracy.
