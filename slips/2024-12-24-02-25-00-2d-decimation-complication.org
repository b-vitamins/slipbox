#+TITLE: 2D Decimation Complication
#+FILETAGS: :physics:spof:ch6:rg:decimation:2d:proliferation:
#+SETUPFILE: ~/.config/emacs/setup/setupfile.org

The transition from one to two dimensions fundamentally alters the decimation procedure, transforming exact renormalization into an approximation problem. Consider the square lattice divided into two sublattices for b=√2 decimation. Removing a spin that couples to four neighbors generates interactions among all four, destroying the nearest-neighbor structure preserved in one dimension.

The four-spin interaction arising from decimation takes the explicit form:

\[R(\sigma'_1,\sigma'_2,\sigma'_3,\sigma'_4) = \sum_{s=\pm 1} e^{K s(\sigma'_1+\sigma'_2+\sigma'_3+\sigma'_4)} = 2\cosh[K(\sigma'_1+\sigma'_2+\sigma'_3+\sigma'_4)]\]

The symmetry of this expression reveals that all four spins interact equally—not just through original nearest-neighbor bonds but also through new diagonal interactions. The proliferation becomes explicit when attempting to maintain Boltzmann form:

\[R = \exp[g' + K'(\sigma'_1\sigma'_2 + \sigma'_2\sigma'_3 + \sigma'_3\sigma'_4 + \sigma'_4\sigma'_1 + \sigma'_1\sigma'_3 + \sigma'_2\sigma'_4) + K'_4\sigma'_1\sigma'_2\sigma'_3\sigma'_4]\]

Six two-spin couplings emerge: four from the original square plaquette plus two diagonal interactions. Additionally, a four-spin coupling K'₄ appears, representing genuinely new physics absent in the original Hamiltonian.

Each subsequent RG step compounds the proliferation. Decimating spins from the renormalized lattice generates six-spin, eight-spin, and increasingly complex interactions. The number of coupling constants grows exponentially with iteration number, rapidly becoming computationally intractable. This explosion of parameters represents the central challenge of real-space renormalization in dimensions greater than one.

The interaction proliferation in higher dimensions is not merely a technical complication but reflects the fundamental difference between one-dimensional chains and higher-dimensional networks. In one dimension, removed spins leave behind simple linear structures. In two dimensions, removed spins create loops—closed paths that support collective behavior and phase transitions. The diagonal interactions generated by decimation are precisely what the Migdal-Kadanoff approximation discards, while the Niemeijer-van Leeuwen approach treats them perturbatively. Both approximations succeed because these proliferating couplings, while numerous, prove largely irrelevant near criticality.
